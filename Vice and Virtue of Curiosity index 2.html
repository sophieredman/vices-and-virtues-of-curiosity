One of the more endearing of the seventy-eight treatises which make up the Moralia of Plutarch is one entitled ‘On not minding your own business’. The Greek title reminds us momentarily of Plato’s famous definition of justice in Republic, which is to do your own thing. Plutarch was indeed an ardent Platonist, but here he is concerned not with political philosophy but with social habits. The treatise reminds me of nothing so much as of a famous Lancastrian comedian of my youth called Norman Evans, who in a sketch called Over the Garden Wall assumed the transvestite role of a nosy female neighbour, simultaneously pegging out clothes and retailing juicy items of gossip. For Plutarch, after defining this nosiness as ‘an unhealthy and harmful state of mind, a fondness for learning the misfortunes of others, a disease apparently free of neither envy nor malice’, condemns the common tendency to pry into the social origins of neighbours, their debts, and their private conversation. Such inquisitiveness, says Plutarch, is invariably accompanied by a wagging tongue, for what these people gladly hear, they gladly blab about. Pascal in his Pensées says much about the same thing: “Curiosity is only vanity. Most often we only wish to know in order to talk about it.”

In New Orleans, almost any native or long-term resident will recognize the verb pike, possibly related to peek, meaning 'to be nosy, to pry, to look or watch with great curiosity.' One usually pikes at his neighbours from behind half-closed blinds or semi-transparent curtains. He, or more likely she, wishes to spy on the neighbours without being seen. The word also exists as a noun, and an adjective has been made from it. For example, one might say to such a busybody, 'Oh, don't be such a pike!' Or he might comment, 'I wonder what those pikey neighbours are looking at.' As generally used, the term clearly has derogatory overtones. Although pike is quite common in colloquial usage in New Orleans, I have never found an outsider who recognizes this meaning. Conversely, every New Orleanian I have spoken to, including one past eighty, has readily defined the term, and some attempt to specify the exact neighbourhood - usually 'downtown' (down river from Canal Street) -where the word is most common.

In the more general sense of 'to look, see, or observe,' pike has been several times recorded. The American Thesaurus of Slang prints the word three times in this sense, twice as general slang and once as underworld slang. The Dictionary of American Underworld Lingo lists the word twice. As a noun, pike is defined as 'a look; a glance,' and the editors give the following example: 'Take a pike at that box (safe) for a bug (hidden alarm) before you lather it up (make the soap ream to hold explosives).' It appears also as a verb, pike off, 'to watch, often surreptitiously, as in preparing for robbery or blackmail.' The example is 'Plant (hide) and pike the sucker off when he stashes (hides) the chunk (money).' None of these examples, however, quite expresses the New Orleans meaning. Interestingly enough, an entry in the EDD comes closest in both sense and connotations. Here the word, as used in Nottinghamshire, is defined 'to look closely and curiously,' with the example: 'She went peepin' and pikin' round the house.'

The only American example I have found that clearly expresses the New Orleans meaning occurs in Eudora Welty's story 'Petrified Man.' Here an important character is named Mrs. Pike. Identified as a native of New Orleans, this character is the perfect pike, forever sticking her nose in other people's business. Repeatedly, we are told that 'she's very observant,' that 'she's got a sharp eye out, Mrs. Pike has.' A real busybody, she observes all the others without herself being seen, learns all their business, and gossips about it. Since it has been several times demonstrated that Miss Welty often gives significant names to her characters and since she certainly has wide knowledge of New Orleans, it seems reasonable to assume that we have here at least one instance of the word pike in a sense that it is all but unknown outside that city.

To look curiously,

VIRTUE

4.

Baumgarten, Elias

“Curiosity as a Moral Virtue”

International Journal of Applied Philosophy, Volume 15, Number 2, 2001

Like other virtues, curiosity takes different forms that make it hard to classify neatly. Curiosity can be a character trait or a desire. As a character trait, curiosity is a disposition to want to know or learn more about a wide variety of things. The more one has this character trait, the more often or the more intensely one will on particular occasions experience a desire or urge to investigate and learn more about something. Though non-human animals are often characterized as exhibiting curiosity, in its human form curiosity as a desire involves choice and judgment, the choice to select some things rather than others as objects for investigation and the judgment that something is worth investigating. We can also speak of a person’s having the capacity for curiosity. If it is possible to speak of some objects as genuinely worthy of investigation (just as we speak of some things as beautiful), then different people may have varying capacities to recognize a thing’s worth as an object of investigation (just as we distinguish among people’s ability to recognize beauty). Finally, a person who experiences curiosity about something may or may not act on the desire or urge which the curiosity “arouses.” We may make moral judgments about both what kinds of things are worthy of one’s curiosity and about occasions on which one should or should not act on curiosity once it is aroused.

Curiosity as a want to learn more,

A character trait, we make moral judgements about what kinds of things are worthy of one’s curiosity

VIRTUE

5.

Baumgarten, Elias

“Curiosity as a Moral Virtue”

International Journal of Applied Philosophy, Volume 15, Number 2, 2001

Curiosity about the world is clearly related to the desire to know, yet it has never ranked with philosophy, the love of wisdom, or the “love of learning” as a noble character trait. When curiosity is directed inward, we associate it with self-reflectiveness, and the value of the “examined life” is familiar, in different ways, to philosophers and writers of university catalogues. Although it, too, deserves more study, I will put aside an assessment of inwardly directed curiosity and focus on “curiosity about the (external) world.” Here we do speak of (and often praise) “intellectual curiosity,” which suggests love of learning, but we also refer to idle curiosity, morbid curiosity, and the notion of curiosity as meddlesomeness. Curiosity also can take the form of voyeurism, of which we generally disapprove. Even in its more positive form, we are unlikely to speak of someone’s being “curious” (even intellectually curious) about the meaning of life or the nature of her moral obligations to aging parents. Though we would not call this usage mistaken, curiosity typically has less elevated objects; it most often focuses on contingent facts about the world. Such investigation may be part of a larger search for wisdom, but it need not be.

Desire to know, love of wisdom, curiosity as a noble character trait, curiosity about oneself is self-reflected, intellectual curiosity, love of learning

VIRTUE

6.

Baumgarten, Elias

“Curiosity as a Moral Virtue”

International Journal of Applied Philosophy, Volume 15, Number 2, 2001

Here is a concrete example of curiosity and its opposite. I was once on an overnight ferry from Izmir to Istanbul, and I met an American soldier who was on leave from his base near a Turkish village. Jeff was from a small town in Nevada and had not been outside the United States before his military service. He shared with me his fascination with all he was learning about Turkish culture and the Turkish people. He made every effort to visit the village near his base, to talk with people, eat their food, and learn about their way of life. He felt lucky that the army had given him this opportunity to learn about another part of the world, but he told me that the other soldiers found his interest in the Turks to be bizarre. After all, there was no need to deal with those foreigners. The base provided everything soldiers needed, and most had no desire to venture outside their comfortable Americanized community. Jeff was curious to learn about an unfamiliar part of world. His fellow soldiers were not. Nothing Jeff said suggested that his interest in Turkish culture was part of a larger search for wisdom. Nonetheless, I would claim that curiosity, such as that expressed by Jeff, is a moral virtue. Though curiosity is not a necessary feature of any well-lived life, it is a quality that is generally conducive to it.

Anecdotal evidence, fascination of learning, curious to learn, curiosity in this example is a moral virtue, curiosity is not necessary feature of life but is a quality that is favourable to it.

Curiosity also empowers other "virtues of thought" such as intelligence. Aristotle defines intelligence as the ability to deliberate wisely about the nature and conduct of a good life. It is an activity, not a passive accumulation of information, and it is tied to one’s character and capacity for goodness: "we cannot be fully good without intelligence, or intelligent without virtue of character" (Aristotle 1985, VI: 13). Intelligence is not merely intellectual aptitude, but a capacity for moral development as well; excellent thought needs excellent action. Curiosity underlies all virtues of thought; it opens the door to wisdom, understanding, and intelligence and makes visible the possibilities for "living well." Though many scholars will insist that reason acts with a value-free objectivity, thinking is laden with moral values. As an intellectual virtue, then, curiosity is unavoidably moral

Virtues of thought, intellectual intelligence, curiosity is an activity rather than a passive accumulation of info.

The fact is that everyone wonders what other individuals do, feel, and think in private; they wonder what others are like when no one is around. The passionate spectator, Baudelaire wrote, "is an ‘I’ with an insatiable appetite for the ‘non-I’.” The idea of unmasking our fellow humans also possesses a vaguely mysterious appeal. At one time, New Yorkers were purchasing up to a hundred telescopes a week. Most of these terrestrial astronomers, a shopkeeper told writer Bill Buford, "are not going home to count the moons around Uranus." What they are doing, he said, is taking advantage of the "comfort distance”, the space between one's apartment and perceptually adjacent ones. It is a distance that, if sufficient, lulls occupants into thinking they are alone and, if the optically assisted viewer is lucky, acting accordingly. Buford saw this behaviour as an expression of our "insatiable humanity, an appetite for more and more about the human species, the visual equivalent of gossip.” For that reason, he wrote, it is bad manners to close the drapes.

9.

Locke, John L.

Eavesdropping: An Intimate History. 

United Kingdom,

OUP Oxford, 2010.

The drive to invade the private spaces of others is universal. The English term “eavesdropping” derives from the practice of standing under the eavesdrop - the place where rain falls from the roof to the ground - in order to hear conversations occurring within the home. In French, to eavesdrop is couter aux portes, to listen at doors, as is the Italian equivalent, origliare alla porta. In Spanish, escuchar sin ser visto means to listen without being seen. In Tzotzil, a language spoken in the Mexican Highlands, there is a verb that means "to observe in secret, from a hiding place." In German, horchen means to listen at the door secretly. In Swedish, an eavesdropper is a tjuvlyssnare, basically a listen-thief; in Polish, podsluchiwanie implies a violation of privacy, by placing oneself "underneath the private conversation." In all these cultures, people have chosen to name this particular temptation: that of listening secretively, especially to speech. But there is no reason why perceptual invasions should not include activity that is seen, smelled, or touched. A great deal of social monitoring is accomplished by eye. Several years ago, the managers of a private club near Washington, DC put silk screens, ficus trees, and potted plants in its spacious dining room to increase a feeling of intimacy. Club members objected. “Now I can’t see who’s on the other side of the dining room,” one woman complained. The deeper motive, of course, is not merely to see the outer surfaces of others. It is to enter and explore the interior, and to inspect the more privileged material that is kept there.

 

Everyone wonders what other people do in private– nosiness, voyeurism – buying telescopes to pry into peoples houses, wanting to know what goes on behind closed doors. Evolutionary trait in everyone, eavesdropping – using eyes and ears to find out more

A "peep" may seem a small thing, defined as "to peer slyly or secretly; take a hasty, furtive look." The "peep" is hasty, just taking a moment. It is furtive, suggesting that he or she is doing something shameful or blameworthy. "Peep" is further defined as "a look through a narrow aperture . . . into a larger space." In the physical world, that can mean the Peeping Tom who stares out at Lady Godiva. In our computerized world, to "peep" means to look through your computer screen into the large expanses of modern databases.

There is a profound ambivalence about how seriously we should treat peeping. The motives to peep are as varied as human nature - to see a handsome or beautiful person, gossip with friends about what you have seen, use the information against your foes, sell the gossip for cash, and perhaps even blackmail someone. As understandable as the impulse is, however, the word "peep" also refers to "furtive" and thus blameworthy activity. As we will see, the penalty to Peeping Tom himself was very severe – a lifetime of blindness. When the foundational story for a phrase imposes such a severe penalty, then we have an important clue that something important is at stake.

 

Peeping, peering, furtive glances – suggesting that it is shameful or blameworthy, motives for peeping

The next step beyond just looking ("the gaze") is to tell someone what you saw ("the gossip"). I resist the law professor's impulse to develop a universal theory of gossip. For our purposes, we first recognize that gossip can cause more types of harm than the gaze. When an individual gazes upon the nude form of Athena or the titillating facts in a celebrity's medical files, he is invading the privacy of the object of the gaze. When the individual tells others, however, additional harms may result to the object of the gaze. The object's reputation may be damaged, with embarrassing results: "Did you know that so-and-so has such-and-such a condition!?" The gossip might spread, leading to loss of employment, denial of insurance, being cast out of a social circle, or other concrete harms.

 

Even Jewish law recognized the harms of gossip, which in Hebrew is l'shon hara or the "evil tongue." The term is synonymous with slander and evil gossip. Jewish religious leaders equate the harm of gossip, a "heinous crime," to that of murder and idolatry. Rabbis recognized that l'shon hara harmed three individuals: he who told it, he who heard it, and he who was slandered. One commentator, poignantly remarked, "If [the Rabbis] were horrified by 1'shon hara in their day, when news took months or years to circulate, consider how they would react today, when words are flashed around the world in an instant.”

 

Gossiping, effects of gossiping on victim and harms, evil tongue

Literary evidence suggests that the historical Godiva probably never actually made the naked ride to save the people of Coventry. Contemporary historians offered detailed chronicles of events of the day, but they said nothing about Godiva's supposed ride, so it seems reasonable to suppose that she wasn't an exhibitionist at all. And Peeping Tom didn't make an appearance in the various retellings of the story until years later. Even if Peeping Tom is fictitious, concerns about voyeurism persist. Men have been known to leer at women. Such behaviour violates religious, cultural, and legal norms. Depending upon how it is done, it can be harmful, manipulative, deceptive, or disrespectful. Each of these gives us some reason to think peeping Toms everywhere should be considered at least a little suspicious. Some will continue to claim that voyeurism is wrong only if someone is harmed. However, few of us would want video cameras in our showers even if no harm was done to us and we remain unaware of another's peeping. Perhaps this is because voyeurism is so often sneaky, and deception can infect the act of looking. There's a fine line between a flirt and an ogle. Much depends on the nature of the gaze. It is one thing to look. It is another to leer, covet, or crave.

 

Peeping Tom, voyeurism, leering at women, harms of ‘peeping’, deception and the act of looking.
